Seniors in NYC

Two stories of lived experiences by NYC seniors

Scrollytelling PSET by Azury, Barbara, and Tanvi
Spring 2020

Why a 10-minute walking distance?

In addition to New York having long been a place famous for the young professionals and temporal dwellers, it also has a lot to offer for the aging population. Sociology professor, Andrew A Beveridge, notes that based on 2015 census data, more than 18,000 people over the age of 60 had moved to New York City from other parts of the States and overseas. However, the middle class and seniors are pushed out as they struggle with the high costs of living in the city. What we can learn is that living in a neighborhood where daily goods and service are reachable within a walkable distance or by public transit is a primary need for the seniors whose mobility and travel distance decrease significantly as they age.





Looking back at the history of urban planning, walkable neighborhoods were first proposed by Clarence Perry in 1920. This was later used as a guideline for planning a self-sustained residential neighborhood.

In Yang and Diez-Roux’s research, they suggest that most people are willing to walk an average 10 mins – which covers 0.5 miles.

This is a highly debated number as the willingness to walk for longer is influenced heavily by the perceived comfort and safety of the walking environment.

However, this is the number widely used for most transit planning in America. Adults over 65 walk slower on average compared to younger folks, with speeds ranging from 0.6m/s to 1.3m/s.

Yang and Diez-Roux also found that seniors walk the shortest distances but for the longest durations, presumably because of their slower speeds.

Senior’s mobility declination has been a progressive result of automobile oriented culture across American Cities. Even though more recent land use policies have made it possible to drive less, few of them address the needs and mobility choice of the older population.



Why does building an equitable city for seniors matter?



80% boomers prefer aging in place, residing at where they are instead of moving to a senior facility. (Find more proof of this one) AARP’s survey also reveals that 60% of Americans over 50 desire living within one mile of daily resources.

Those who live independently as well as those who reside at Senior Apartments rate the importance of a walkable neighborhood almost twice as highly as having access to public transit. Seniors across different income brackets express desirability to live closer to essential facilities. Walkable neighborhoods are also a critical metric that impact seniors’ independence and health in multiple studies.

Building an age-friendly city will become ever more relevant to the growing senior population in New York City in the next 20 years, as the population of 65 and over will grow by 41% in 2040 (citation?). Investing in walkable neighborhoods will have the added benefit of reducing car ridership and carbon emissions resulting from a car-oriented city.

Case Study 1



Meet Ms. Fischer, 71 years old, a linguist from San Diego.

























She resides in a 2 bedroom apartment in the Upper West Side.

























“You save a lot by not needing a car. Everything that’s important in my life is within walking distance. Lincoln Center is across the street. My doctor is six blocks away. My dentist is nearby. Who needs a car?”

























She is one of the seniors who enjoys one of the most walkable neighborhoods in Manhattan.
